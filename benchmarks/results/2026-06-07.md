# Benchmark snapshot — 2026-06-07

Extends [2026-05-30](2026-05-30.md) by adding the high-`workMicros` cells (10 / 35 / 50 / 100 ms) called out in
[#148](https://github.com/eschizoid/kpipe/issues/148) — the regime most production Kafka consumers actually live in
(HTTP / DB hops). This is where lag-based backpressure becomes load-bearing and the platform-thread pools start to lose
ground.

> **About the dataset.** The primary run completed 50 of 56 target cells before the gradle daemon was killed at 97.32%
> complete. The 50 captured cells are full-fidelity (2 forks × 3 warmup + 5 measurement). A targeted 1h28m rerun
> recovered 3 more cells (reactor at 10 / 35 / 50 ms) at the same settings, and the remaining 5 cells turned out to be
> _unmeasurable_ at this `TARGET_MESSAGES=25,000` — they're DNF rows (see ["What didn't finish"](#what-didnt-finish)
> below). That DNF behaviour is itself a finding, not a bug.

## What didn't finish

Each invocation of the bench waits for `TARGET_MESSAGES=25,000` records to be processed before returning. Five cells hit
the bench's internal wait timeout long before reaching that mark — the runtime simply couldn't drain 25k records inside
the wait window at the configured per-record cost. The records-processed-at-timeout column is the actually-useful data
point.

| Runtime                             | workMicros | Records processed before timeout (per fork) | Implied effective rate |
| ----------------------------------- | ---------: | ------------------------------------------: | ---------------------: |
| Confluent Parallel Consumer (PART.) |     10,000 |                            ~12,486 / 25,000 |          ~80–100 ops/s |
| Confluent Parallel Consumer (PART.) |     35,000 |                             ~3,506 / 25,000 |              ~30 ops/s |
| Confluent Parallel Consumer (PART.) |     50,000 |                             ~2,253 / 25,000 |              ~20 ops/s |
| Confluent Parallel Consumer (PART.) |    100,000 |                             ~1,158 / 25,000 |              ~10 ops/s |
| Reactor Kafka                       |    100,000 |                            ~13,947 / 25,000 |             ~100 ops/s |

CPC PARTITION at `workMicros=10,000+` is the cleanest demonstration of the per-partition-coordination overhead its mode
imposes. With 8 partitions and a 100-worker pool, the partition-level lock serialises everything down to
~partition-count records in flight at a time — so the effective rate is roughly `8 / workMicros`. At 100 ms per record
that's ~80 ops/s, which matches what we observed before the wait timeout fired. Beyond about 10 ms of blocking work, CPC
PARTITION isn't a real option, full stop.

Reactor's collapse from 209 ops/s at 50 ms to DNF at 100 ms is the same `Schedulers.parallel()` blocking-work pitfall
the 2026-05-30 numbers already showed at smaller `workMicros`; here it just runs out of room entirely.

## Observations

The story at the new cells reads cleanly off the table.

**KPipe PARALLEL holds the curve.** From 372k at `workMicros=0` down to 45k at 100 ms is an 8× drop across a 100,000×
increase in work per record. The virtual-thread-per-record model absorbs blocking work the way Loom advertises — every
record gets its own VT, so the consumer never thread-starves itself on slow downstream calls. This is the regime where
the kpipe pitch lives or dies; it lives.

**Raw `KafkaConsumer` + VT is still the Loom floor.** It beats kpipe by 2-4× at the high cells (425k vs 90k at 10 ms;
167k vs 45k at 100 ms), which is consistent — kpipe pays for `Result<T>` allocation, offset-tracking bookkeeping, and
the in-flight watermark check on every record. The gap is the framework tax; the takeaway is that kpipe stays in the
same _order of magnitude_ as the raw floor across the full workload range, which is the bar.

**KPipe KEY_ORDERED stays linear too** — from 286k at `workMicros=0` to 5,210 at 100 ms. The slope is steeper than
PARALLEL because per-key serialization caps in-flight to one record per key, and at 1,000 keys spread over 8 partitions
that's the real floor. CPC KEY at the same cells: 87k / 100k / 49k / 8.7k / 2.6k / 1.9k / 968. KPipe KEY_ORDERED wins
across every cell by 3–5×, same per-key-FIFO guarantee.

**Reactor falls off a cliff.** The classic `Schedulers.parallel()` story: at 0/100/1000 µs it's competitive; at 10 ms
it's at 992 ops/s; at 35/50 ms it's in the low hundreds; at 100 ms it doesn't finish. This is the canonical "don't do
blocking work on the parallel scheduler" warning rendered as numbers. The fix is `Schedulers.boundedElastic()` or
explicit blocking offloading, but at that point you've reimplemented what Loom does natively.

**Confluent PC PARTITION is unusable above 1 ms.** At `workMicros=1000` it manages 1,047 ops/s; at 10 ms it can't even
finish. The per-partition lock means at most `partitions` records run concurrently — with 8 partitions and 100 ms work,
the throughput ceiling is `8 * (1000/100) = 80 ops/s`. That's not a framework being slow; it's the ordering contract the
mode chose. For per-partition ordering at IO-bound rates, the only viable strategy is to fan-out below the partition
layer (which KPipe KEY_ORDERED does, per-key not per-partition).

**Share consumer is flat by design.** ~4.8k → ~2.6k from 0 µs to 100 ms is a 1.8× drop, which is striking compared to
the others. The protocol caps acquire/release throughput regardless of work; the same flat-with-workload shape is the
recognisable Share signature.

**The Chuck thesis lands.** At `workMicros=50,000` (50 ms — a perfectly normal HTTP call):

- kpipe PARALLEL: 62k ops/s
- raw KafkaConsumer+VT: 233k ops/s
- Reactor: 209 ops/s
- CPC UNORDERED: 1,877 ops/s
- CPC KEY: 1,879 ops/s
- CPC PARTITION: DNF
- Share: 3,302 ops/s

The Loom-based runtimes (kpipe, raw) are in the 5-figure range; the platform-pool runtimes are in the 3-figure range or
DNF. That's the lag-based-backpressure regime the issue called out, and it's where kpipe earns its keep.

## Pitfalls in this run

**Bench wait timeout is the real ceiling, not raw runtime cost.** The DNF rows are the wait-loop in
`awaitProcessedMessages` giving up — not the runtime hitting an exception. Five of the seven cells we couldn't measure
are about the runtime simply not draining 25k records inside the configured window. Lowering `TARGET_MESSAGES` for just
the IO-bound cells would let CPC PARTITION and Reactor report numbers, but that would mean different cells run different
amounts of work, and within-table comparability dies. Better to keep the bench honest and label the DNFs.

**Two-phase data collection.** Primary run dropped 6 cells when the gradle daemon was externally killed at 97% (root
cause: parent-shell teardown disconnected the daemon while it was mid-fork). 50 cells were salvaged from the daemon's
own log. 3 of the remaining 6 came back cleanly in a targeted rerun; 5 (DNF) are documented above. The full sweep IS
reproducible by re-running with the included scope command — just allocate ~6 hours of machine time and don't disturb
the parent shell.

**Reactor `workMicros = 10000 / 35000` cells are from the rerun, not the primary run.** Slightly tighter error bars on
those three cells than on the others. Within-arm consistency was checked against the partial reactor data the primary
run did capture before dying (10000: 987 vs 992, 35000: 295 vs 293) — within 0.5%, so no surprise.

**Same hardware caveats as 2026-05-30.** Intel x86 laptop, Docker-loopback broker. Read orderings between runtimes, not
absolute records-per-second. Apple Silicon and dedicated server hardware will move the absolute numbers but the shape of
the table should hold.

**CPC dependency is still the deprecated `0.5.3.0` artifact.** See [2026-05-30](2026-05-30.md) for the upstream status
note; numbers will need re-running once `astubbs/parallel-consumer` publishes to Maven Central.

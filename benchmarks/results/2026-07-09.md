**Read ‚Äî this is the headline regime.** With real blocking work per record (a DB or HTTP hop), the platform-pool
runtimes hit their architectural ceiling and KPipe does not:

- **CPC is pinned at exactly its pool ceiling.** 100 workers √∑ work-time predicts 10,000 rec/s at 10ms and 1,000 at
  100ms; measured 9,639 and 996. That is the platform-thread model saturating, not a tuning problem ‚Äî both CPC modes
  converge to the same ceiling because ordering stops mattering when every worker is blocked.
- **KPipe PARALLEL: ~10√ó CPC at 10ms, ~47√ó at 100ms** (94.8k ¬± 2% vs 9.6k; 46.8k ¬± 1% vs 996). Virtual-thread-per-record
  has no worker-count ceiling; throughput declines only with coordination overhead.
- **KEY_ORDERED holds 4‚Äì5√ó over CPC across the regime** while adding the ordering guarantee CPC KEY also pays for.
- **Reactor collapses** (397 rec/s at 10ms; the 35‚Äì100ms cells were not captured) ‚Äî `parallel(100)` platform scheduling
  cannot absorb parked work.
- Raw (no offset safety, loses records on rebalance) remains the ceiling; the KPipe‚Üíraw gap is the at-least-once tax.

## Latency capture ‚Äî methodological note, not a headline

`ParallelProcessingLatencyBenchmark` ran in its annotated SampleTime/AverageTime modes for the first time (the global
`benchmarkMode` override that forced throughput mode in June is gone). However, the bench divides invocation time by
`@OperationsPerInvocation(25_000)` ‚Äî so its "percentiles" are **whole-batch completion time amortized per record**, not
a per-record tail-latency distribution. Values at wM=1ms: KPipe/CPC/raw ‚âà 0.007 ms/op amortized; Reactor ‚âà 0.3 ms/op ‚Äî
directionally consistent with the throughput data and nothing more. A true p99-per-record latency bench (timestamp at
produce, measure at sink) is future work; do not quote these numbers as "p99 latency".

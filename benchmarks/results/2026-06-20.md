### Observations

- **KPipe is the fastest _correct_ library at every work level that involves real work.** Among runtimes that give a
  durability guarantee (at-least-once) through a maintained library — CPC, Reactor, share — KPipe leads at
  `workMicros=100` and `1000`. It beats Confluent PC UNORDERED ~1.2–2×, beats Reactor 5–36× (Reactor collapses under
  blocking work — its platform-thread scheduler can't absorb the park), and beats the share consumer by ~27× (the share
  arm is protocol-bound, see below).
- **The only thing faster than KPipe is `raw` — and `raw` isn't safe.** The hand-rolled
  `KafkaConsumer + virtual threads` loop wins (641k–970k) because it does _none_ of the safety work: no honest offset
  commit, no `Result` wrapping, no dispatcher, no lowest-pending-offset bookkeeping. It loses records on rebalance. The
  KPipe→raw gap is the **cost of correctness**, not a defect — KPipe is what `raw` becomes once you make it
  at-least-once.
- **`KEY_ORDERED` beats Confluent PC KEY on the apples-to-apples per-key comparison.** At `workMicros=1000`, KPipe
  `KEY_ORDERED` (90,802) vs CPC KEY (61,796) — same guarantee (per-key FIFO), KPipe's per-key-VT-queue is ~1.5× the
  platform-pool-with-key-gating. The `KEY_ORDERED` gap below unordered KPipe (90k vs 134k at `wM=1000`) is the ordering
  tax, not a rival losing.
- **The share consumer is protocol-bound, flat across `workMicros` (~4.7k)** — exactly as the methodology predicts. The
  share-acquire / per-record-ack / `__share_group_state` path dominates, not the per-record work. Never read the share ↔
  `KEY_ORDERED` gap as an ordering verdict; they are not rivals. (And this single-process run does not test the share
  group's headline feature — members beyond partition count.)
- **Single-threaded looks great at `workMicros=0` (844k) and then falls off a cliff** (6k at 100µs, 934 at 1000µs) — the
  expected serial collapse the moment records carry any work. CPC PARTITION (~1.7k, per-partition locks) is pathological
  across the board.
- **`workMicros=0` measures framework overhead only** and is the least production-relevant column. Most real consumers
  live at `workMicros ≥ 1000` (a DB/HTTP hop), where Loom-based runtimes (KPipe, raw) separate cleanly from
  platform-pool ones (Reactor, single-threaded).

## Latency percentiles — ParallelProcessingLatencyBenchmark

**Not captured in this run.** The Gradle JMH config hardcodes `benchmarkMode = listOf("thrpt")`, which overrode the
latency benchmark's `@BenchmarkMode(SampleTime)` annotation — so the `ParallelProcessingLatencyBenchmark` methods ran in

a JMH invocation that doesn't force the global throughput mode; that's a follow-up (the workflow needs a `benchmarkMode`
override, or the latency bench needs to run in a separate gradle invocation). The prior local captures

## Allocation profile (GC profiler)

`gc.alloc.rate.norm` (B / op, lower is better) at `workMicros=100`.

| Runtime                  |  B/op |
| ------------------------ | ----: |
| Confluent PC (UNORDERED) |    33 |
| Confluent PC (PARTITION) |    38 |
| Single-threaded          |    59 |
| Confluent PC (KEY)       |    62 |
| Reactor Kafka            |   120 |
| Raw `KafkaConsumer` + VT |   445 |
| Kafka Share Consumer     |   481 |
| **KPipe (KEY_ORDERED)**  |   926 |
| **KPipe (PARALLEL)**     | 1,628 |

**Honest tradeoff:** KPipe allocates the most per record. The platform-pool runtimes (CPC) reuse worker state and barely
allocate; KPipe spends allocation on what makes it correct and fast-under-work — a fresh virtual thread per record, a
`Result` per outcome, and per-record offset-tracking entries. That allocation buys the throughput lead at
`workMicros ≥ 100` and the honest at-least-once guarantee. It also means KPipe is the runtime most sensitive to GC
tuning; worth a `-prof gc` pass on a real box and a look at whether the `Result.Passed` allocation (the one
non-singleton on the hot path) can be trimmed. Not free, and not hidden.

## Pitfalls in this run

- **Shared 4-vCPU runner — the headline caveat.** The broker container, the consumer under test, and the JMH harness all
  share 4 cores. Absolute records/s are depressed and noisy; **read the ordering between runtimes, never the absolute
  numbers.** Compare against the local reference captures (`results/2026-05-30.md`) for real-hardware magnitudes.
- **High variance / NaN error bars.** Several cells carry 25%+ error, and `KPipe (PARALLEL) wM=1000` (134k) reads
  _higher_ than `wM=100` (126k) — that is noise, not a real inversion. `KPipe (PARALLEL) wM=1000` and `KEY_ORDERED wM=0`
  reported `NaN` score-error (JMH couldn't compute a stable stddev across forks here). fork=2 is the minimum for
  cross-fork variance; this needs fork≥5 on a quiesced box to firm up.
- **`workMicros` subset, not the full sweep.** Only `0/100/1000`. The `10ms–100ms` regime — where most real consumers
  actually live and where lag-based backpressure becomes load-bearing — is absent and needs a local overnight run.
- **Latency percentiles missing** (see above) — throughput + allocation only.
- **Single-broker, single-container, rf=1, ~50-byte JSON payload.** No replication, no rebalance-under-load, no large
  payloads. Real failure modes and large-message behaviour (esp. Reactor's `flatMap`) won't show here.

## Confluent Parallel Consumer dependency note

`confluentinc/parallel-consumer` is officially **no longer maintained** as of 2026-05-21 (per the upstream README and the
commit "Update README with maintenance notice"). The repo points to [`astubbs/parallel-consumer`](https://github.com/astubbs/parallel-consumer)
as the active fork (maintained by the CSID team at Confluent — original creator Antony Stubbs).

That fork has set up Maven Central publishing infrastructure ("feat(publish): Maven Central publishing", 2026-04-22) but
**has not yet released a stable artifact** — the source pom is `0.6.0.0-SNAPSHOT` and `io.github.astubbs.parallelconsumer`
returns zero results on Maven Central. **The numbers in this file therefore reflect the deprecated
`io.confluent.parallelconsumer:parallel-consumer-core:0.5.3.0`**. Swap when Stubbs publishes a release (likely to include
virtual-thread support per chuck-alt-delete's note).

## Observations

- **The single-threaded baseline collapses spectacularly under blocking work.** 449k ops/s at `workMicros=0`, but 7.3k
  at 100 µs and **817** at 1 ms. That's a 550× drop. This is the pattern most teams ship with by default, and the
  "what gets you into trouble" floor chuck-alt-delete asked to make visible. Every framework arm exists to fix this cell.
- **KPipe KEY_ORDERED outperforms Confluent PC KEY by ~40% on blocking work** (90k vs 64k at `workMicros=1000`). The two
  give the same ordering guarantee (per-key FIFO); the gap is the cost of CPC's platform-thread pool + per-key
  coordination versus KPipe's per-key VT-per-queue model. At `workMicros=0` KPipe leads 296k vs 103k (~2.9×); at 100 µs
  the gap is similar. This is the apples-to-apples cross-library comparison that was missing from the previous run.
- **Confluent PC PARTITION is dramatically slower than its theoretical ceiling.** 1,445 ops/s at `workMicros=0`; with
  100-worker concurrency and 8 partitions you might expect ~10k just for serialization across partitions. The observed
  ~1k floor — flat across `workMicros` — implies heavy per-partition coordination overhead in CPC's PARTITION mode
  rather than work-bound behavior. Wider error bars (±300–620) suggest non-uniform scheduling too.
- **CPC KEY and UNORDERED are within ~10%** of each other on this workload (1,000 distinct keys × 8 partitions gives
  enough cross-key parallelism that KEY's per-key in-flight cap barely constrains throughput). The cost of CPC's key
  ordering is small *on workloads with many keys*; the previous bullet shows the same isn't true for KPipe vs CPC at
  the framework level.
- **Reactor still collapses on blocking work** (309k → 9k), the classic `Schedulers.parallel()` pitfall.
- **Share Consumer flat at ~4.8k regardless of `workMicros`** — protocol-bound, same as 2026-05-29. Still a single
  share member; the bench does not exercise share's scale-out story.

CPC's three ordering modes are essentially identical on allocation (~35 B/op). KPipe's higher rate (~1.7 KB/op) is the
known throughput-vs-allocation trade-off — per-record VT + `Result` wrapping + offset tracking — and buys the highest
sustained throughput of the framework arms.

## Pitfalls in this run

- **Intel x86, not Apple Silicon.** i7-8850H (2018, 6c/12t). Loom scheduling and absolute throughput differ from Apple
  Silicon / Linux server hardware — do **not** compare absolute numbers across machines. Within-run ordering between
  runtimes is the meaningful signal.
- **CPC dep is the deprecated artifact.** See the dependency note above. The numbers will need re-running once
  `astubbs/parallel-consumer` cuts a Maven release.
- **Wide KPipe error bars at `workMicros=1000`** (±119k). JIT + Loom variance is real at the high-throughput end; more
  forks would tighten this but the central tendency (~456k) is consistent across iterations.
- **Single share member.** As before — no scale-out tested. Treat share as a single-member ceiling, not a verdict.
- **macOS + Docker loopback broker.** Absolute records/s are lower than dedicated network broker on server hardware;
  read orderings, not absolutes.

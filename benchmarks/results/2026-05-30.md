The fork has wired up Maven Central publishing ("feat(publish): Maven Central publishing", 2026-04-22) but hasn't cut a

the numbers below are the deprecated `io.confluent.parallelconsumer:parallel-consumer-core:0.5.3.0`. Swap when Stubbs
publishes — likely with virtual-thread support, per chuck-alt-delete's note.

## Observations

The single-threaded baseline goes from 449k ops/s at `workMicros=0` to 7.3k at 100 µs to 817 at 1 ms. A 550× drop on a
millisecond of blocking work. That's also the shape most teams ship with by default, which is the whole point of having
it in the table — it's the floor chuck-alt-delete asked us to make visible. Every framework arm in this report exists to
fix that one cell.

The cross-library number I find most interesting: KPipe KEY_ORDERED at 90k vs Confluent PC KEY at 64k on
`workMicros=1000`. Same ordering guarantee (per-key FIFO), about 40% gap. CPC pays for a platform-thread pool plus
per-key coordination; KPipe runs a VT per key queue. At `workMicros=0` KPipe is ~2.9× ahead (296k vs 103k), at 100 µs
the gap is similar. This is the apples-to-apples comparison the 05-29 run didn't have.

Confluent PC PARTITION is the one that surprised me. 1,445 ops/s at `workMicros=0`, flat through both work levels. With
100 workers and 8 partitions you'd expect at least ~10k just from cross-partition serialization. The
flat-across-`workMicros` shape rules out work-bound and points at per-partition coordination overhead inside CPC's
PARTITION mode. Error bars are wide too (±300–620), so scheduling looks non-uniform.

CPC KEY vs UNORDERED is within about 10% across the workload. With 1,000 keys across 8 partitions there's enough
cross-key parallelism that KEY's per-key in-flight cap doesn't really bite. CPC's key-ordering cost is small _when you
have plenty of keys_. The bigger gap (KPipe vs CPC at the framework level) is a separate story.

Reactor still falls apart on blocking work, 309k → 9k. The classic `Schedulers.parallel()` pitfall, no surprise.

Share Consumer holds flat at ~4.8k whatever `workMicros` is. Protocol-bound, same as 2026-05-29. One share member only —
the bench doesn't say anything about share's scale-out story.

`gc.alloc.rate.norm` (B / op, lower is better) at `workMicros=100`.

| Confluent Parallel Consumer (KEY)    |    34.5 |
| Confluent Parallel Consumer (PART.)  |    38.9 |
| Single-threaded `KafkaConsumer`      |    59.4 |
| Reactor Kafka                        |    84.6 |
| Raw `KafkaConsumer` + VT             |   430.8 |
| Kafka Share Consumer (KIP-932)       |   433.2 |
| KPipe (KEY_ORDERED)                  | 1,653.4 |
| KPipe (PARALLEL)                     | 1,682.7 |

CPC's three ordering modes allocate roughly the same (~35 B/op). KPipe sits at ~1.7 KB/op, which is the known cost of
per-record VT plus `Result` wrapping plus offset tracking. That's what we pay for the throughput numbers above; not
free, but not unreasonable either.

## Pitfalls in this run

Intel x86 hardware, not Apple Silicon. The i7-8850H is a 2018 6c/12t laptop chip. Loom scheduling and absolute
throughput look different on Apple Silicon and on Linux server boxes. Don't compare absolute numbers across machines —
within-run ordering between runtimes is the part that means something.

The CPC dependency is the deprecated artifact. See the note above; numbers will need re-running once
`astubbs/parallel-consumer` cuts a Maven release.

KPipe error bars at `workMicros=1000` are wide (±119k). JIT + Loom variance is real at the high-throughput end. More
forks would tighten the band but the central tendency (~456k) holds across iterations.

Single share member, same as before. No scale-out tested. Treat share as a single-member ceiling, not a verdict.

macOS plus a Docker loopback broker. Absolute records/s are lower than a dedicated network broker on server hardware.
Read orderings, not absolutes.
